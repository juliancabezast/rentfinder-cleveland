How to Rent a House with a Pet in Cleveland (Deposits & Fees)

Ohio has no law specific to pet deposits, so landlords set their own pet deposit and pet rent amounts. Any refundable pet deposit is generally treated as part of the security deposit under ORC 5321.16 — meaning the same 30-day return and itemization rules apply when a tenant with a pet moves out.

Updated July 4, 2026 ·6 min read ·By the Rent Finder Cleveland team

Can a landlord charge a pet deposit in Cleveland?

Yes. Ohio has no statute written specifically for pet deposits, so a landlord is generally free to require one as a condition of allowing a pet in a rental home. What Ohio law does regulate is what happens to that money afterward: a refundable pet deposit is treated the same as any other security deposit under ORC 5321.16, the state statute covering deposit returns statewide.

In practice that means a landlord can ask for an additional deposit specifically tied to a pet, on top of a standard security deposit, but once it's collected it's still subject to the same return rules as any other deposit money — it isn't a separate, unregulated category of payment. Renters with a pet should ask upfront whether the deposit is refundable and how it will be itemized at move-out.

Pet deposit vs. pet rent — what's the difference?

A pet deposit is typically a one-time, refundable amount collected before move-in, meant to cover pet-related damage beyond normal wear and tear. Pet rent is a separate, non-refundable amount added to the monthly rent for as long as the pet lives in the unit — it functions more like an amenity fee than a deposit.

Because pet rent is rent, not a deposit, it is not covered by ORC 5321.16's deposit-return and interest rules — it simply becomes part of what's owed each month. Some Cleveland-area landlords use one structure, some use the other, and some combine both, so it's worth asking exactly how a listing labels its pet charges before signing.

The label matters more than renters sometimes realize. A $300 pet deposit that's fully refundable at move-out (assuming no damage) is a very different financial commitment than $30 a month in pet rent added indefinitely, even though both might show up in a listing simply as "pet fee: $300." Ask the leasing agent to spell out, in writing, whether a charge is refundable, recurring, or both, and get that detail added to the lease itself rather than left as a verbal understanding from the showing.

How does Ohio deposit law apply to a pet deposit?

Ohio law generally applies the same statewide rules to a pet deposit as to any other refundable security deposit, since ORC 5321.16 does not carve out a separate category for pet-related funds. What can a landlord deduct from a pet deposit?

Ohio law generally allows deductions only for actual damage caused by noncompliance with lease terms or statutory tenant duties (ORC 5321.05) — not for ordinary wear and tear. A carpet worn down over years of normal living is different from carpet damage from an unaddressed pet accident; only the latter is a lawful deduction.

Documenting the unit's condition at move-in with photos or a written checklist protects both sides. Our move-in inspection checklist walks through exactly what to record before you move a pet in.

It also helps to walk the yard, not just the interior, before bringing a pet home — note the condition of any fencing, gates, or landscaping at move-in, since those areas often come up in pet-related deduction disputes. If a lease requires a fenced yard to be kept in a certain condition, get that requirement in writing rather than assuming a verbal description at the showing covers it.

Are service and assistance animals treated like pets?

No. Under the federal Fair Housing Act, disability is a protected class, and a trained service animal or a documented assistance/emotional-support animal is generally not treated as a "pet" for deposit or fee purposes — a landlord generally cannot charge a pet deposit or pet rent for a legitimate assistance animal used as a disability accommodation. This is a separate legal category from a pet policy and should be discussed directly with the leasing office rather than assumed from a listing's stated pet rules.

Because this is a reasonable-accommodation request rather than a standard pet application, it's typically handled outside the normal pet-deposit conversation — renters who need an assistance animal accommodation should raise it directly when applying rather than waiting until after a lease is signed and a standard pet policy has already been applied.

Frequently asked questions

Is there a legal limit on pet deposits in Ohio?
No. Ohio law does not cap the amount a landlord can charge as a pet deposit or pet rent — that's set by the individual landlord or property manager. What the law does regulate is how a refundable pet deposit must be returned: within 30 days of move-out, itemized, and with interest above certain thresholds under ORC 5321.16.
Do I get my pet deposit back when I move out?
You should, minus any documented pet-related damage beyond normal wear and tear. Ohio law generally requires landlords to return deposit funds — including pet deposits — within 30 days of lease termination and provide a written itemized statement for anything withheld.
Can I be charged both a pet deposit and pet rent?
Yes, some Cleveland-area landlords charge both: a one-time refundable deposit plus an ongoing monthly pet rent. There's no Ohio law preventing this combination — it depends on what the individual lease specifies, so read the pet clause carefully before signing.
Can a landlord charge a pet deposit for a service animal?
Generally, no. Service animals and documented assistance animals are treated as a disability accommodation under the federal Fair Housing Act, not as pets, so pet deposits and pet rent typically don't apply to them. Renters should raise this directly with the leasing team rather than assume a standard pet policy applies.
