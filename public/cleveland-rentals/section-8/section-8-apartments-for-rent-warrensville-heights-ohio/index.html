Warrensville Heights' voucher protection, and how it compares

Ohio has no statewide law requiring a landlord to accept a Housing Choice Voucher, and neither does the City of Cleveland — Cleveland's 2024 "Pay to Stay" ordinance dropped source-of-income provisions during negotiation before it passed. Warrensville Heights is different: reporting describes it, along with Cleveland Heights, South Euclid, and University Heights, as one of a small group of Cuyahoga County suburbs that has adopted its own local protection. That's a meaningful distinction from neighboring suburbs like Garfield Heights and Maple Heights, which are not reported to have equivalent ordinances.

How the CMHA voucher process works

Whether or not you end up renting inside Warrensville Heights, the voucher itself comes from CMHA, the Cuyahoga Metropolitan Housing Authority, which administers Section 8 for the entire county. CMHA's preliminary application is open online year-round with no closing date, and applicants are chosen from that list through periodic random mini-lotteries rather than strict order — a name might come up within days, or it may take months, according to CMHA's own guidance. Once a household holds a voucher, the specific unit still has to pass a housing-quality inspection; HUD is transitioning that standard from HQS to NSPIRE, with a final deadline of February 1, 2027 for the Housing Choice Voucher program.

Getting around Warrensville Heights

Warrensville Heights has no rail station of its own, but it sits just south of the Green Line's eastern terminus near Green Road, on the Shaker Heights/Beachwood border, giving nearby residents rail access into Shaker Square and Downtown's Tower City hub. RTA's regular bus network also runs through Warrensville Heights directly along Warrensville Center Road, connecting to both the Blue and Green light-rail lines.
